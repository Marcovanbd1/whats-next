The question nobody answers

Every day, millions of NHS appointments end the same way.

The clinician has done their job. The patient walks out.

And then what?

She's been to see her GP about not sleeping. Anxiety, probably. The GP was kind. The prescription is in her bag. She's standing in the car park. Still anxious. Still exhausted. Nobody told her about the group on Thursday evenings. Or the community worker two streets away who does exactly this. — Sarah. Every surgery. Every day.

For as long as the NHS has existed, the clinical encounter has had an answer. The what next? has not.

The moment of highest need

She's sitting on a hospital bed. Discharge letter in her hand. She doesn't know how she's getting home. Not enough money for a taxi. Can't remember where the bus goes from. Too exhausted to think who to call. Her daughter's at work. The nurse is busy. The ward is loud. Nobody is coming. — Margaret. Every ward. Every discharge.

The what-next problem is at its most acute the moment someone leaves hospital — overwhelmed, often alone, holding a letter that tells them what happened but not what to do next.

Why a playlist — not a library

When you make someone a mixtape, you don't hand them a library. You sequence something for where they are right now.

The distinction

A library says: everything is here, go find it. A playlist says: I heard you. Here's what I think you need, in this order, starting now.

Most of what follows a clinical encounter is a library. A leaflet. A website. A signposting sheet built for someone who arrived ready — with a plan, a next step already forming.

Most people don't arrive ready. They arrive in a state. And they leave in one.

What Support Playlists actually are

Not a chatbot. Not a portal. Not a directory with a filter.

A one-shot system. Natural language in. Curated, sequenced support out. No login. No account. No personal data stored.
